Welcome!

With this first post of Agro Blog, I’m going to do some basic exploration of a FAO dataset (FAOSTAT Statistical Database 2020) which includes measures of production, yield, and area harvested for the whole list of crops and countries recorded, with observations ranging from 1961 to 2018.

In this exploration, I will go through my steps of working with this dataset in R, including the cleaning/tidying process, exploratory analysis, and visualization.

For the purposes of this post, I will examine the 2018 data for both the United States and Peru (the two countries where I have explored agriculture). After cleaning and tidying the data, for each country I will take a look at the top 10 crops by tonnage of production, and compare this with the top 10 crops by yield. For a crop of interest, I will examine how the country stacks up against worldwide production, and how those production numbers have changed over the time period represented by the data.

This data is not ready to use just yet - all of the years are included as column names (and with that messy “Y”). To fix this, I will use a pivot to bring all of those years into a single column “Year”.

crops <- raw_data %>% pivot_longer(cols = starts_with("Y"), names_to = "Year", names_prefix = "Y", 
    values_to = "value", values_drop_na = TRUE)

Now, with this Table 5 of the top ten corn-producing countries, let’s see how their production has changed since 1961.

Figure 1: Corn Production

Examining Figure 1 above, we can see that production in both the United States and China has increased dramatically in the last half-century, with Brazil making considerable growth as well. The parallel growth of the US and China suggests the presence of some oligopolistic conditions in the global corn market - those countries had already been established as production leaders, making it difficult for other countries to inject significant competing production.

This dataset does not include any information on domestic vs. foreign consumption for each crop item - an analysis with that data would better inform my opinion on the economic implications of these production data. It would help me answer questions such as “Do the small corn producers produce less because their current production already satisfies domestic consumption, or because the global commodity market forces them out?”

Figure 2: Potato Production

There is a lot more variation in this graph, compared to the time-series graph of corn production in Figure 1. China’s leadership in this global market is a much more recent development than the dominance of United States corn in the previous graph. Again, these results leave me desiring consumption data to address the other dimension of this market. In particular, I’d like to find out whether Peru is a net importer or exporter of potatoes, since the commodity is so valuable to the population. A FAO report (The Global Potato Economy 2008) cites the perishability and high transport costs of potatoes, so I would doubt much significant trade between Peru and countries like China and India. I will likely pursue further investigations of this topic in future posts.
